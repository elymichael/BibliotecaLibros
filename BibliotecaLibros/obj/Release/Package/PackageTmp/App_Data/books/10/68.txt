
"Ah, thou dost remember!"

"My memory mendeth, thou seest. &#160;Set thy mind at ease—thy
back shall go unscathed—I will see to it."

"Oh, thanks, my good lord!" cried the boy, dropping upon his
knee again. "Mayhap I have ventured far enow; and yet—"

Seeing Master Humphrey hesitate, Tom encouraged him to go on,
saying he was "in the granting mood."

"Then will I speak it out, for it lieth near my heart.
&#160;Sith thou art no more Prince of Wales but King, thou canst
order matters as thou wilt, with none to say thee nay; wherefore it
is not in reason that thou wilt longer vex thyself with dreary
studies, but wilt burn thy books and turn thy mind to things less
irksome. Then am I ruined, and mine orphan sisters with me!"

"Ruined? &#160;Prithee how?"

"My back is my bread, O my gracious liege! if it go idle, I
starve. &#160;An' thou cease from study mine office is gone thou'lt
need no whipping-boy. Do not turn me away!"

Tom was touched with this pathetic distress. &#160;He said, with
a right royal burst of generosity—

"Discomfort thyself no further, lad. &#160;Thine office shall be
permanent in thee and thy line for ever." &#160;Then he struck the
boy a light blow on the shoulder with the flat of his sword,
exclaiming, "Rise, Humphrey Marlow, Hereditary Grand Whipping-Boy
to the Royal House of England! &#160;Banish sorrow—I will betake me
to my books again, and study so ill that they must in justice
treble thy wage, so mightily shall the business of thine office be
augmented."
